Review Comments on "Trends and patterns of healthy life expectancy among the elderly in China"
12月 25 2023

Review comments, 2023, Chinese Jorunal of Sociology, “Trends and patterns of healthy life expectancy among the elderly in China”.

This article illustrates that the healthy life expectancy of Chinese older adults does not adhere to a linear trajectory; instead, it exhibits fluctuations. Specifically, it aims to discern whether the increase in healthy life expectancy arises from reduced mortality rates or improved health rates, thereby augmenting our understanding of the fluctuations in healthy life expectancy more comprehensively.
Throughout this article, several recommendations are presented for consideration, aimed at refining and enhancing specific aspects of the manuscript.
Firstly, focus on refining the discussion concerning the measurement outcomes derived from both census data and CLHLS data, which exhibit differing yet somewhat analogous results, as illustrated in Table 3 and Figure 6. Thus, in the “Discussion and conclusion” section on page 24, paragraph 2, the statement concerning the decline in health rates among the elderly, emphasized as a major contributor to the decrease in healthy life expectancy, appears somewhat conclusive. For enhanced accuracy, it is advisable to confine this statement to the CLHLS data. Also, it is recommended to include a brief comparison with census data in the section titled “The effect of mortality and health rates on healthy life expectancy from 2011 to 2018” on page 22.
Secondly, a more precise paragraph division is suggested. This article delineates five explanatory mechanisms for the variation in healthy life expectancy: rapid economic growth, development of medical facilities, unhealthy lifestyles resulting from urbanization, the establishment of a health insurance system, and the challenge of sample representativeness. The content in the second paragraph on page 24, focusing on the adverse impact of rapid short-term economic growth on health, overlaps with the subsequent discussion on page 25, referencing Shi and Hu (2010). Consider enhancing the exposition concerning urbanization and unhealthy lifestyles or alternatively, integrating the discussion on urbanization with the discourse on economic growth.
Thirdly, please provide further clarification on the health status indicator based on ADL. In the CLHLS data, the health status, based on ADL (ranging from 6 to 18 points) is classified into two categories: “capable of self-care” and “partially or completely incapable of self-care”. Since scores 7 to 17 necessitate varying degrees of assistance, this classification implies an overlap. Does this imply that if an individual respondent scores 3 points in any of the 6 ADL questions, they will be categorized in the latter group? Otherwise, they will be classified in the former category.
Fourthly, please consider simplifying the display charts. In Figure 1, the downward trend of males aged 60-64, based on census data, is challenging to discern. Consider adjusting the coordinate axis scale and ensuring consistency with the display method utilized in CHARLS data. In Figure 2, considering the paper’s focus on ages 65-69, 80-84, and 100+, and recognizing that the table provides detailed data for other age groups, simplifying or merging the charts might be advantageous.
