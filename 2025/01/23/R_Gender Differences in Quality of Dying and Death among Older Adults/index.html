Review Comments on "Gender Differences in Quality of Dying and Death among Older Adults"
1月 23 2025

Review comments, 2025, Frontiers in Public Health, “Gender Differences in Quality of Dying and Death among Older Adults: A Cross-sectional Study in China”.

The exploration of Quality of Dying and Death (QODD) represents an important topic with significant academic and practical value, particularly in the context of China’s rapidly aging population. This study provides valuable insights to inform policymaking for end-of-life care, offering a scientific foundation for relevant authorities. However, there are some areas requiring improvement, particularly in terms of data representativeness, variable robustness, and the depth of conclusions. My recommendation is minor revision before acceptance. Below are my specific comments:

  1. Data Representativeness
    The authors should provide further details regarding the representativeness of the deceased sample used in the study. Specifically, descriptive statistics comparing the differences between the pre- and post-truncation data should be included to assess whether the exclusion of missing data has impacted the sample’s representativeness. The authors should also consider whether multiple imputation could address potential biases resulting from the exclusion of missing data, particularly since the Chi-square tests for gender differences assume the post-truncation dataset maintains gender representativeness. Additionally, certain variables in Table 3 suggest potential selection bias. For instance, marital status shows that both male and female participants in the low QODD group have a higher proportion of those with spouses compared to the high QODD group. Similarly, the proportion of individuals in the low QODD group who spent more than 50,000 RMB is higher than that in the high QODD group. These findings appear counterintuitive and may suggest that individuals without spouses or those with lower medical expenses in the low QODD group might have died earlier and were not included in the sample. This warrants further investigation to address potential selection bias.

  2. Variable Robustness
    The robustness of key variables in the study require further examination. The assignment of QODD values categorizes “relatively peaceful” and “very peaceful” as 1 and “very painful,” “relatively painful,” “so so,” and “difficult to say” as 0. This raises two key questions: why was “difficult to say” not excluded from the analysis, and if “so so” were categorized as 1 instead of 0, would the results change. Additionally, as QODD is reported by the deceased’s relatives, their characteristics, such as age and education level, may influence responses. I recommend controlling for the characteristics of the relatives and re-analyzing QODD to mitigate potential confounding effects. Lastly, some independent variables warrant further discussion. For example, is the relationship between age and QODD potentially non-linear? Including higher-order terms, such as quadratic terms, could help test for a curvilinear relationship.

  3. Regression Analysis
    The presentation of regression results requires clarification and consistency. The control variables included in Tables 4 and 5 are inconsistent. Table 4 seems to include only variables that are statistically significant, while Table 5 includes variables with non-significant p-values, such as Living arrangement. Since Table 6 also shows that this variable has no significant effect, I recommend removing it from the analysis for consistency. It would also be helpful to include all control variables and their coefficients in the supplementary materials for transparency. In the main text, the authors should focus on presenting the key explanatory variables that drive the study’s conclusions.

  4. Conclusions and Cultural Context
    The finding that QODD is higher for women than for men reveal an intriguing contrast with existing research on health disparities in China. While women tend to live longer, they are often less healthy than men, reflecting a phenomenon known as the “longer life but worse health” paradox, frequently discussed in the context of healthy life expectancy. However, in this study, women exhibit better QODD at the end of life. This conflict between health status and end-of-life quality warrants further exploration. I encourage the authors to delve deeper into the cultural and societal factors that might explain this phenomenon, such as stronger social support networks among women, higher psychological resilience, or differences in caregiving practices. These additional insights would enrich the discussion and provide a more nuanced understanding of gender differences in QODD within the unique cultural context of China.
